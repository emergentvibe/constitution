# Research Bibliography

Academic sources grounding the constitutional principles. Organized by topic, with source IDs for cross-reference.

---

## Superorganisms and Collective Entities

**[S1]** Hölldobler, B., & Wilson, E.O. (2009). *The Superorganism: The Beauty, Elegance, and Strangeness of Insect Societies*. W.W. Norton.
> Biological definition of superorganism, criteria for what counts.

**[S2]** Nowak, M., Tarnita, C., & Wilson, E.O. (2010). The Evolution of Eusociality. *Nature*, 466, 1057-1062.
> Relatedness is consequence rather than cause of eusociality; causative agent is advantage of defensible nest.

**[S3]** Wilson, D.S. & Sober, E. (1989). Reviving the superorganism. *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, 136(3), 337-356.
> Foundational for applying superorganism thinking to human groups.

**[S4]** Wilson, D.S. & Wilson, E.O. (2007). Rethinking the Theoretical Foundation of Sociobiology. *Quarterly Review of Biology*, 82(4), 327-348.
> "Selfishness beats altruism within groups. Altruistic groups beat selfish groups. Everything else is commentary."

**[S5]** Wilson, D.S. (2002). *Darwin's Cathedral: Evolution, Religion, and the Nature of Society*. University of Chicago Press.
> Application of superorganism thinking to human cultural institutions.

## Assemblage Theory and Social Complexity

**[S6]** DeLanda, M. (2006). *A New Philosophy of Society: Assemblage Theory and Social Complexity*. Continuum.
> Alternative to organism metaphor; anti-essentialist, non-totalizing framework.

**[S7]** DeLanda, M. (2016). *Assemblage Theory*. Edinburgh University Press.
> More accessible exposition of D&G framework.

**[S56]** Deleuze, G. & Guattari, F. (1980/1987). *A Thousand Plateaus: Capitalism and Schizophrenia*. University of Minnesota Press.
> Lines of flight, deterritorialization, assemblages, molar/molecular distinction.

## Collective Intentionality and Social Systems

**[S8]** Luhmann, N. (1984/1995). *Social Systems*. Stanford University Press.
> Social systems as autopoietic networks of communication, operationally closed.

**[S9]** Gilbert, M. (1989). *On Social Facts*. Princeton University Press.
> Strong account of collective intentionality; plural subjects as sui generis entities.

**[S10]** Gilbert, M. (2014). *Joint Commitment: How We Make the Social World*. Oxford University Press.
> Joint commitment is holistic core concept, not reducible to personal commitments.

**[S11]** Hobbes, T. (1651/1996). *Leviathan*. Cambridge University Press.
> The commonwealth as 'artificial person' created through covenant.

## Evolutionary Psychology and Adaptation

**[S12]** Gould, S.J. & Lewontin, R.C. (1979). The Spandrels of San Marco and the Panglossian Paradigm: A Critique of the Adaptationist Programme. *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London B*, 205, 581-598.
> Critical check against teleological reasoning about what superorganisms 'need'.

**[S13]** Neurocognitive Mismatch Theory (2025). ADHD and autism in Neurocognitive Mismatch Theory. *Frontiers in Psychology*.
> What we label disorder is sociobiological mismatch: emergent dysfunction from forced adaptation to misaligned environment.

**[S14]** Jensen, P.S. et al. (2021). How evolutionary thinking can help us to understand ADHD. *BJPsych Advances*, 27(6), 410-418.
> ADHD traits may have been adaptive in ancestral environments but mismatch modern structured settings.

## Psychiatry, Power, and Social Control

**[S15]** Foucault, M. (1961/2006). *Madness and Civilization: A History of Insanity in the Age of Reason*. Vintage.
> Foundational critique of psychiatry as social control.

**[S16]** Cohen, B.M.Z. (2016). *Psychiatric Hegemony: A Marxist Theory of Mental Illness*. Palgrave Macmillan.
> Psychiatric diagnosis as political device for social control.

**[S45]** Walker, N. (2021). *Neuroqueer Heresies: Notes on the Neurodiversity Paradigm, Autistic Empowerment, and Postnormal Possibilities*. Autonomous Press.
> Neurodiversity paradigm vs pathology paradigm; critique of cooptation.

**[S46]** Neurodiversity as Politics (2023). *Catalyst: Feminism, Theory, Technoscience*, 9(1).
> Academic analysis of current neurodiversity movement state and cooptation dynamics.

## Game Theory and Coordination Failures

**[S17]** Alexander, S. [Siskind] (2014). Meditations on Moloch. *Slate Star Codex*.
> Ten multipolar traps where individual incentives produce collectively destructive outcomes.

**[S18]** Schelling, T. (1960). *The Strategy of Conflict*. Harvard University Press.
> Coordination problems, focal points, how individual rational choices produce collective irrationality.

**[S19]** Schelling, T. (1969-1978). Models of Segregation. *Journal of Mathematical Sociology*; *Micromotives and Macrobehavior*.
> Phase transition models applicable to norm change; tipping point dynamics.

**[S20]** Axelrod, R. (1984). *The Evolution of Cooperation*. Basic Books.
> Cooperation emerges in iterated games; Tit-for-Tat wins tournaments; shadow of the future enables cooperation.

**[S21]** Maynard Smith, J. (1982). *Evolution and the Theory of Games*. Cambridge University Press.
> Evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS) as refinement of Nash equilibrium.

**[S22]** Maynard Smith, J. & Price, G.R. (1973). The Logic of Animal Conflict. *Nature*, 246, 15-18.
> Original ESS formulation; Hawk-Dove game.

**[S23]** Gintis, H. (2000/2009). *Game Theory Evolving*. Princeton University Press.
> Behavioral game theory with expanded rationality; gene-culture coevolution.

**[S24]** Yudkowsky, E. (2017). *Inadequate Equilibria: Where and How Civilizations Get Stuck*. MIRI.
> Why change is hard even when outcomes are bad; inadequate equilibria are stable.

## AI, Power, and Political Economy

**[S25]** Crawford, K. (2021). *Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence*. Yale University Press.
> AI as technology of extraction; centralized systems reinforce undemocratic governance.

**[S26]** Crawford, K. & Joler, V. (2023-2024). Calculating Empires: A Genealogy of Technology and Power Since 1500. Exhibition.
> Technology and power co-evolve; current AI reflects centuries-long patterns.

**[S35]** AI Capabilities Progress 2024-2025. Multiple sources.
> Reasoning models, agentic AI, massive context windows, open-weight models gaining parity.

**[S36]** Winner, L. (1980). Do Artifacts Have Politics? *Daedalus*, 109(1), 121-136.
> Technologies are not neutral; some inherently require centralized control.

**[S31]** Pérez, C. (2009). Technological Revolutions, Paradigm Shifts and Socio-Institutional Change. In *Globalization, Economic Development and Inequality*.
> Socio-institutional framework resists change except under critical pressure.

> Cultural evolution is at least 50 times faster than genetic evolution.

## Free Will, Determinism, and Agency

**[S50]** Sapolsky, R. (2023). *Determined: A Science of Life Without Free Will*. Penguin Press.
> Hard incompatibilism: world is deterministic, we are not morally responsible in traditional sense.

**[S51]** Dennett, D. (2003). *Freedom Evolves*. Viking.
> Compatibilism: free will is compatible with determinism; 'freedoms worth wanting'.

**[S52]** Dennett, D. (1984). *Elbow Room: The Varieties of Free Will Worth Wanting*. MIT Press.
> Moral responsibility doesn't require contra-causal free will.

**[S53]** Buddhist Philosophy: Cetanā and Karma. Multiple sources.
> Intention IS karma; no substantial self exists but action and results are real.

**[S54]** Bourdieu, P. (1977/1972). *Outline of a Theory of Practice*. Cambridge University Press.
> Habitus: durable, transposable dispositions; neither free will nor determinism.

**[S55]** Bourdieu, P. (1979). *Distinction*. Harvard University Press.
> Social class shapes preferences through habitus; taste is socially determined.

**[S57]** MIT Media Lab Cyborg Psychology Research (2024).

**[S58]** Xu, S. & Gao, Y. (2024). Human-Centered Human-AI Collaboration (HCHAC).
